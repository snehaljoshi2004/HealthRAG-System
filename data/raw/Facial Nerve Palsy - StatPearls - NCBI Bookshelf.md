# Facial Nerve Palsy - StatPearls - NCBI Bookshelf

## Section

The distinction between central and peripheral facial palsy is crucial. In a peripheral facial nerve lesion (e.g., Bell's palsy, lesion after the stylomastoid foramen), the entire ipsilateral half of the face is affected, resulting in inability to wrinkle the forehead, difficulty closing the eye (lagophthalmos), and mouth deviation. In contrast, a central facial lesion (e.g., stroke affecting the motor cortex) typically spares the forehead as the upper face receives bilateral cortical innervation
